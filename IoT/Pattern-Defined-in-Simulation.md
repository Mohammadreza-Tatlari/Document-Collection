
### Patterns Defined in The Simulation Scenarios

1. Pattern 1: Irrigation decisions are *not* optimization problems 
    - Decisions happen **earlier than optimal** (fear of cutoff).
    - Over-irrigation is preferred to under-irrigation
    - “Sleep better” shows up repeatedly as a value proxy.
Precision is secondary to **regret minimization**.

2. Pattern 2: Time uncertainty > quantity uncertainty </br>
Farmers tolerate not knowing *how much* water they applied, but they hate not knowing *when* water will arrive or stop.
Timing predictability is higher value than volumetric accuracy.

3. Pattern 3: Most constraints are social or institutional, not technical </br>
Any system assuming **full local autonomy** is misaligned.

4. Pattern 4: Consequences of mistakes are delayed and ambiguous </br>
    Feedback loops are slow and noisy. This explains **low tolerance for new systems** that introduce uncertainty.
    - Learning is hard.
    - “Trial and error” is expensive and discouraging.
    - Farmers avoid experimentation.

5. Pattern 5: Trust is observational, not credential-based </br>
    Proof must be **local, physical, and comparative**.
    - Trust elders, neighbors, visible results.
    - Distrust officials, vendors, abstract advice.
    - “Seen in nearby fields” beats any report or dashboard. </br>

6. Pattern 6: Money sensitivity is nonlinear and contextual </br>
    **Pricing models matter as much as feature sets.**
    - One-time capex tolerated more than subscriptions.
    - Anything recurring feels predatory.
    - “Small” expense thresholds vary but are psychologically rigid.
    - Reliability is valued but rarely paid for explicitly.

7. Pattern 7: Stress clusters around *responsibility without control* </br>
Farmers are held responsible for outcomes they cannot fully control.
Stress is cause by accountability mismatch. </br>
Tools that increase *responsibility* without increasing *control* will be rejected.

8. Pattern 8: There are three hidden layers in every scenario </br>
Every irrigation decision sits on:
1. **Physical layer** – water, soil, pumps (IoT focused)
2. **Coordination layer** – neighbors, schedules, labor
3. **Legitimacy layer** – rules, blame, social acceptance </br>
**Ignoring coordination & legitimacy guarantees failure in shared-resource contexts.**

9. Pattern 9: Information is valuable only if it reduces blame </br>
Farmers don’t just want to know—they want to **defend decisions**. Data is used socially, not analytically. </br>
Decision support must also be **justification support**.

10. Pattern 10: Adoption friction ≠ lack of tech literacy </br>
It’s **fear of asymmetric downside**. Adoption requires **downside protection**, not upside promises. </br>
The asymmetry makes:
- Free trials risky
- Pilots unattractive
- “Experimental” positioning toxic

11. Pattern 11: MVP value is scenario-selective, not universal </br>
Solutions Require ecosystem or coordination-level interventions. One MVP cannot span all scenarios honestly.

12. Meta-pattern: what *doesn’t* show up (important)
    Farmers are not rejecting tech; they are rejecting **tech-shaped problems**.
    - Very little excitement about “automation”
    - Minimal desire for dashboards
    - Almost no talk of “data ownership”
    - No language of “optimization” or “AI”

#### Concolusion
- Any solution must:
  - Reduce uncertainty *before* decisions
  - Respect social dynamics
  - Avoid recurring costs
  - Provide locally verifiable proof
  - Protect farmers from downside risk

- Any solution that:
  - Starts with sensors
  - Leads with precision
  - Assumes individual autonomy
  - Uses SaaS pricing
  - Frames value as yield %

is structurally misaligned for most scenarios you explored.
